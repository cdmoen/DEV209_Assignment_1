Experiments with
Calcined Alumina

Testing calcined alumina additions in cone 6 porcelain glazes.
Exploring how different percentages affect surface texture and opacity in oxidation firing.

This series documents my experiments with calcined alumina in cone 6 oxidation glazes. The base recipe used is Old Forge First Five Floating Cobalt with 2.5% titanium dioxide and 0.6% cobalt added. Calcined alumina can significantly impact glaze opacity, texture, and surface quality when added to porcelain glazes. The tests show percentages ranging from 0% to 15% calcined alumina.

Test Progression

Below are the individual test tiles with descriptions of how each percentage of calcined alumina affects the glaze:

0% Calcined Alumina

The control glaze with no calcined alumina added. This shows the baseline transparency and surface quality of the base glaze recipe before any alumina modifications.

2% Calcined Alumina

With 2% calcined alumina, there is little to no change in appearance (the apparently darker color is simply due to poor lighting).

4% Calcined Alumina

At 4% calcined alumina, there is still very little change in appearance. There will be a marked shift once we get to 6%, implying that very subtle effects would begin to manifest in the 4-6% range.

6% Calcined Alumina

6% calcined alumina creates a significant shift toward opacity. The surface texture becomes a satin matte, and the color is much lighter.

8% Calcined Alumina

At 8% the color is even lighter, but the most notable change is in the tendency for the glaze to start breaking toward a jade green color on sharp edges where the glaze is extremely thin. This effect will increaase with greater percentages.

10% Calcined Alumina

At 10% Calcined Alumina the tendency to break green on sharp edges is even more intense, occurring even where the glaze was applied thickly near the top of the tile.

12% Calcined Alumina

12% is where real visible defects in the surface texture can start to be seen, likely due to the calcined alumina increasing the melting temperature of the glaze and causing the tile to under fire. Pinholes can be seen as a result of air bubbles present in the glaze during application. These normally heal over when a glaze melts thoroughly in the kiln, but this glaze is now so refractory that it does not fully melt and allow the surface defects the chance to heal over.

15% Calcined Alumina

At 15% Calcined Alumina, the green color is now present throughout the entire glaze - a result of extreme under firing. The lack of pinholes in this tile is likely due to the fact that its glaze application did not contain any air bubbles, which surely would not have healed over if they had existed at any point.

Conclusion

The addition of calcined alumina, while useful in pushing a glossy glaze toward matte, will prevent the glaze from fully maturing at a certain point, which means there is a risk of leaching. While it is possible to push the glaze partly toward a satin matte and still have acceptable leaching, this can only be trusted if you have your wares tested at a lab to confirm that the leaching of coloring oxides is within acceptable limits, or if you use a nontoxic colorant like iron.
