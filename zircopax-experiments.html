Experiments with
Zircopax

Testing zircopax (zircon) additions in cone 6 porcelain glazes.
Exploring how this opacifier affects transparency and surface quality in oxidation firing.

This series documents my experiments with zircopax (zirconium silicate) in cone 6 oxidation glazes. Zircopax is a powerful opacifier that can significantly increase glaze opacity. The base recipe used is Old Forge First Five Floating Cobalt with 2.5% titanium dioxide and 0.6% cobalt added. The tests show percentages ranging from 0% to 10% zircopax.

Test Progression

Below are the individual test tiles with descriptions of how each percentage of zircopax affects the glaze:

0% Zircopax

The control glaze with no zircopax added. This shows the baseline transparency and surface quality of the base glaze recipe, including the 'milky' mottling effect caused by the titanium dioxide.

2% Zircopax

With 2% zircopax, there is no clouding effect. Instead, it has the unexpected effect of counteracting the 'milky' mottling caused by the titanium dioxide in the control tile, creating a clearer, more uniform surface.

4% Zircopax

At 4% zircopax, the glaze remains largely transparent with no significant clouding. The counteraction of the titanium dioxide's mottling effect continues, though subtle opacifying effects may begin to manifest in the 4-6% range.

6% Zircopax

6% zircopax marks the point where the opacifying effect really starts to become apparent. The glaze shows increased opacity compared to lower percentages, and the color begins to push toward a pastel.

8% Zircopax

With 8% addition, the glaze achieves maximum opacity. The zircopax creates a very opaque, uniform surface.

10% Zircopax

There does not seem to be a marked difference between 8% and 10%, implying that 8% is likely the maximum amount one would add to a recipe such as this.

Conclusion

Zircopax is an effective opacifier for cone 6 glazes, and, as illustrated here, is a good way to push a glaze toward more neutral, pastel colors with uniform consistency.
