Experiments with
Slow Cooling

Exploring the effects of slow cooling on glaze development and surface textures.
Comparing fast fire versus slow cool results in cone 6 oxidation firing.

Magnesium Microcrystals

The following variations showcase the differences between fast-fired and slow-cooled versions of a Selsor Chun 2 glaze with 0.5% Cobalt added. Glazes such as these with high levels of Magnesium and low levels of Calcium can often be turned into satin mattes through a process of surface crystalization when the glaze is allowed to cool slowly.

High-Magnesium Glaze

The left image was fired with a standard E1 electric schedule with no ramp-down, while the image on the right was fired with a very slow cool, with 1-hour holds at 1700 and 1600 degrees Farenheit.

The effect of slow cooling on a Floating Iron Glaze

All three of these tiles used the same recipe adapted from Old Forge Floating Iron, but with only 2% Iron and the addition of 2% Cobalt. The leftmost tile was fired using a standard E1 fast-fire schedule with no ramp-down. The middle tile was fired with a slow cooling rate of 100deg/hr from 1700 to 1200 degrees Farenheit. The rightmost tile was fired with 1hr holds at 1700 and 1600 degrees farenheit, and a slow cooling rate of 50deg/hr from 1600 to 1400 degrees Farenheit. Note the increasing opacity and milky color.
